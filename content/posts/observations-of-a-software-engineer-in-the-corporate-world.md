I have been writing software for more than 12 years now and I have been strongly involved in the corporate world for the last 6-7 years or so, I just wanted to share some things that I constantly notice appearing in the corporate environment where software development is a key factor. There are some lessons to be learned and hopefully, people who are open to learning them.

## Recognizing individual contributions

When things go well, they are awesome, but when they are bad, they are a nightmare. This is the motto of many software development teams. Managers don't tend to recognize individual software engineers who contribute not only to the existing codebase but to the overall software development processes in the company they are working for. Teams are judged as a whole similar to the communist times where if one makes a mistake, the whole team has to pay for it.

This is a huge problem. Because evaluating the team without assessing individual team members leads to some people working a lot and others, not doing their job properly. Meaning some people cover others. And the more time this continues to be this way, the more people you will lose at a certain point of the company lifecycle. This creates stress in the environment, and low self-esteem of the engineers as a whole (because this guy doesn't push as hard as I do but gets the same salary at the end of the day). Not only that but it also shows that the company can foster qualities like laziness and poor code quality without even stopping and thinking if the team is moving forward, has stopped, or is going backward in their development efforts.

## Recognizing contributions for the sake of it

I have seen so many team events where the same members get recognized just because they have loud mouths or they are someone's favorite. This gets to the point not only where it is super ridiculous, but to the point where feelings get hurt. This is because a team is not only a gathering of robots that write code, but a team is a connection between a group of people which is emotional, intellectual, and personal. You foster this connection and that is how you build teams. But if you have one favorite person or a couple of them and you always try to showcase them to others, you will inevitably lose this connection inside the team.

Recognition is not that hard. It is not about money, nor about experiences. It is about connection. Simple words of gratitude are sometimes enough to compensate for weeks of hard effort. And being part of the company's "All Hands" presentation is not what it is about. It is about team events, team spirit, and team connection.

## Replacing people just because you want to get to the next level

All of the efforts in a company are to build great teams. With great teams come great achievements. There is no other way around. People are the ones making the product as good as it gets. If it sucks - people are also responsible for it. From CEOs to managers to software developers. A product is what it is because of the people behind it.

Lots of companies put a huge amount of effort into finding the right people. Sometimes, these processes take months to complete. The last time we were searching for a PO, it took more than a year. After you find good people, you build the team spirit and connection. But now, very often, I witness the company firing people who actually devoted their minds, attention, and sometimes even health to the project. This strong feeling of company using people until they "give everything they got" is super disappointing. I cannot believe that someone in this world believes a human has limitations on what they can achieve. Do you remember to story of Steve Jobs? What limits did he have?

Of course, all of this comes because of the "connections" and "money" thing. Companies want to get rich and recognized fast. They need the right people. But sometimes, the only right people are in your team. Time and the right people are what it takes for a company to become profitable.

## Processes used to control people rather than clear communication

You know the "Empowered" book by Marty Cagan. This was the latest thing we got in one of the projects I was working on. Everyone was so hyped by the "Empowered" way of working where teams will achieve great stuff and CEOs will believe in their team's power ... Well, forget about that bullshit.

Marty Cagan will be mad but I can bet with him that probably 10% of companies are the ones that actually follow his advice. The other 90% are using his book to just hide the CEO's willingness to control the company by directly making decisions that should be strictly followed.

So if a CEO decides he wants this X feature and you tell him that X feature does not make much sense to the team, the CEO will just find someone else to do the X feature and will fire you. Or even worse, you will spend time figuring out all of the team goals and ideas that should be implemented, and then all of this will stay in Confluence or Notion somewhere, and in 5-6 months it will be forgotten while you are doing the N-th feature requested by the CEO.

So all of these fake processes are spreading like a disease. If any of the agile manifesto gurus join even one of the companies I worked with in the last 10 years, I bet they will be holding their heads at what the hell "agile" this is. Probably the best interview question that you can ask is: "What thing you have implemented recently that was inspired by your team?"
